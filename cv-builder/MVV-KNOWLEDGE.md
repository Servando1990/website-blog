1. Investor Types & How They Differ

Family Offices

* Family offices are among the most versatile investors in private markets — they can participate in early VC, late VC, secondaries, credit, and PE funds. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1763164283002719
* Some family offices only participate in private equity buyouts (control deals). Others only do venture; many do a mix including take-privates, pre-IPO deals, and secondaries.
* Family offices are secretive — they often don't disclose check sizes or deal types on their websites. Using N/A as a CRM value is intentional so they still surface in filtered searches. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1763164283002719
* "Direct Investment (Private Equity)" = a catch-all tag for family offices that invest in directs but where we don't know if it's minority or control. Keeps them surfacing in filtered searches even without granular data. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1763164283002719
* Institutional family offices (e.g., MFOs like SCS Financial, Cloverlay) look and behave more like asset managers — formal mandates, portfolio companies, LP positions in institutional funds like Arctos.
* "Private Investment Firm" is often just another label for a family office or independent sponsor — classify as Asset Manager, Family Office, or Independent Sponsor for targeting accuracy.
* For sports deals, enrich family office records with: source of wealth, principal's sports background, presence of other esoteric/uncorrelated assets in their portfolio, and whether they currently have zero sports exposure (may be open to adding). https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775096841236819

RIAs (Registered Investment Advisors)

* RIAs disclose their alternatives allocations publicly via ADV Part 1 and Part 2 filings — this is a key data source for understanding their private market appetite. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1762472423974149?thread_ts=1762295449.253309&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* Schedule D filings reveal what private funds they hold, including fund vintages, types, and whether they are feeders or fund of funds. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1762472423974149?thread_ts=1762295449.253309&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* RIAs can write large checks through their private fund vehicles — look for those with meaningful alternatives allocations.

Fund of Funds (FoF)

* FoFs evaluate co-investments but they will often say "no" to a direct deal (one where they are the only or lead investor) if it's not structured as a co-investment alongside a fund manager. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1762212538818129?thread_ts=1762212538.818129&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* Understanding whether a deal is a co-investment vs. a direct is critical — it determines which investor type will even look at it.

Private Equity (PE) Firms

* PE firms are not appropriate for most deal types Misha works on (9 out of 10 instances). The fee structure of the BD/advisory model does not match PE deal mechanics. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775526011064199?thread_ts=1775504033.331859&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* PE only participates in PE deals — they do not cross into VC. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1763087191921669?thread_ts=1763084814.294879&cid=C09L8TFBJNP

Venture Capital (VC) Firms

* VC only participates in VC, not PE. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1763087191921669?thread_ts=1763084814.294879&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* VCs are not appropriate targets for co-investment deals or SPV-based offerings — do not include them in campaign lists unless the deal is specifically a VC round. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1776106875572629?thread_ts=1776106875.572629&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* Investment Banks are also a no-go for deal campaigns. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775526011064199?thread_ts=1775504033.331859&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* SPACs are not appropriate for Series A deals — SPACs take companies public; they are not a match for early-stage private placements. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775526011064199?thread_ts=1775504033.331859&cid=C09L8TFBJNP

Independent Sponsors

* Independent sponsors are deal-by-deal operators who don't manage a committed fund — they raise capital on a transaction-by-transaction basis.

HNWI (High Net Worth Individuals)

* HNWIs can write checks in the $500K–$2M range — key target segment for ERA/SPV-based co-investment offerings.
* HNWI buckets to target: C-suite executives at Fortune companies, GPs of funds (hedge, PE, credit, VC), lawyers, doctors, real estate developers, founders/senior execs at operating companies (e.g., Factor75, SunBasket, Pepsi). These are senior people who can decide quickly without 6-month diligence cycles. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1772065061473299
* If a person is a partner at a Consumer Fund, assume high consumer interest AND a desire for diversification outside consumer — both signals are useful for targeting.
* For HNWI outreach via ERA/SPV, compliance requirements are lighter — no Smarsh archiving required.
* Personalized language for HNWIs at PE/VC firms: "MVVCM is an ERA designed to partner sophisticated HNWI investors with high-grade institutional deal flow."
* AIDENTIFIED.COM can identify "fans" (e.g., subscribers to sports publications) — useful for identifying sports-oriented individual investors. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775096841236819


2. Deal Types & Structures

Co-Investment vs. Direct Deal

* A co-investment means an investor participates alongside a lead fund/GP — this is what most family offices, FoFs, and RIAs prefer.
* A direct deal (no lead fund) is harder to place — many institutional investors will decline if there's no lead GP/fund co-investing.
* Co-investment can be a sub-type: it can come as part of a control/acquisition OR as part of a growth round — these are different and must be distinguished in the CRM and matching logic. A family office that "likes co-investments" may only do so in a control context, not growth. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765318929337049
* SPV economics vs. transaction fee: determines which investor type to prioritize. If the deal is an SPV/feeder fund → prioritize HNWIs. If it's a transactional fee deal → prioritize larger institutional relationships. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1772056003236299

Deal Stages

* Seed / Series A / Series B / Series C, D, E, F, G = priced venture rounds (equity financing of private companies at defined valuations). https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1764174588816939
* Lower Middle Market (LMM) = typically companies with modest EBITDA and revenue; targeted by control/buyout investors.
* Growth Equity = minority investment in a growing, profitable company — not a buyout.
* Control / Acquisition = investor buys a majority stake or outright acquires a company.
* Pre-IPO = investment in a company shortly before it goes public.
* Secondaries = buying existing LP positions or equity stakes in a fund from another investor.
* Take-Private = acquiring a publicly traded company and de-listing it.
* Distressed / Turnaround = acquisition of a struggling or underperforming company. Requires investors specifically experienced in operational turnaround — a different profile from standard PE or growth investors. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765555797840989
* Venture Debt = lender-only deal type. Must NOT be mixed with equity deal types (Series A/B/C) in targeting logic.

SPV / ERA Structure

* SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle): A legal entity created specifically to pool investor capital for a single deal. Investors buy into the SPV, which then invests in the target company.
* ERA (Exempt Reporting Adviser): A lighter regulatory structure (vs. full RIA) used for investment advisory when managing private funds or SPVs below certain thresholds. MVV Capital Management operates as an ERA. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1774993183798069
* When outreach runs through the ERA/SPV structure, it uses the MVV Capital Management signature (not MVV Capital Partners). https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1776477394169599?thread_ts=1776198296.157059&cid=C09L8TFBJNP

Syndicated vs. Sole Investor Deals

* Some investors have a minimum check size they must deploy — if the round is too small for them, they won't proceed even if the deal is otherwise a fit. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1761247717368229?thread_ts=1761247717.368229&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* Some investors prefer syndicated deals (multiple investors participating). Others prefer being the only investor in a deal.
* Example logic: Round size = $30M, investor tops out at $30M check → they'd be ~1/3 of the equity = generally a good fit. If round is $10M but investor minimum is $20M → they likely won't proceed. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1761247717368229?thread_ts=1761247717.368229&cid=C09L8TFBJNP


3. Check Size Intelligence

* Check sizes are deal-type dependent. The same investor who writes a $30M check into a PE control deal might only write $500K–$1M into a Series A — because of risk/return profile differences. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1764476463264009
* Early-stage companies (Series A) have much higher odds of failure than a profitable company doing $10M EBITDA growing 15% YoY. This risk differential drives lower check sizes at early stages.
* For a family office with a range of e.g. "$10M–$40M": the upper range may require co-investing alongside other family office partners — the investor alone can do $20M but brings in partners to reach $40M.
* Investor check size ranges in the CRM capture everything from venture to PE to funds — the nuances live in the Notes field. Always read notes to understand the conditions behind a check size. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1764476463264009
* Ownership percentage matters: If round size = $120M and an investor tops out at $30M, they'd own ~25% of the equity — a reasonable fit. If a deal is $15M and an investor minimum is $20M → they won't proceed.
* Some investors won't be the sole investor: they require a syndicated deal (other investors co-participating). Conversely, some prefer being the only investor — both preferences exist and must be captured. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1764476463264009
* Deal type must be provided in the matching prompt: explicitly state whether the deal is a growth round, control acquisition, or fund — because the same investor may only write a specific size into each category.


4. Investor Targeting Logic

Deal-to-Investor Matching

* Match investors based on: deal stage, deal type, check size range, industry, geography, EBITDA/revenue thresholds. All must align. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1768432356623179
* Use comparable deals/companies as a targeting signal — look up which investors backed comparable companies and use those as targets.
* Use AI/LLM prompts to identify what financing rounds a specific investor has done — stage, check size, company type. Highly actionable for net-new targeting.
* Firm descriptions and investor websites contain critical targeting data — always enrich from website copy.
* For a co-investment deal, the ideal investor profile is: family offices, RIAs, Fund of Funds, and HNWI individuals (senior PE/VC partners, lawyers, doctors, real estate developers, operating company executives). NOT VCs, NOT PE funds. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1772065061473299
* For a distressed/turnaround deal: find investors that currently own portfolio companies in the same sector (e.g., food manufacturing) — Pitchbook is needed for this level of portfolio-matching. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765555797840989
* Niche prompt example: For Brami (pasta brand, $30M Series B, needs greenfield facility): "Prioritize investors who have experience or background in food manufacturing, specifically greenfield facility development." The ability to add custom instructions at search time is critical. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1767651349564869
* Geography matters: An investor located in Utah has a high probability of being interested in companies in their "backyard." Geography should be a matching signal. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1762209628668389
* Industry parsing from descriptions: Even if no formal "investment criteria" exists, parsing investor website descriptions (e.g., "we look for companies doubling top-line revenue") can produce actionable targeting signals. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1762209476714219
* Investor portfolio stage history is far more useful than check size alone — knowing they participated in Series A, B, C etc. tells you where they invest across the lifecycle.

Exclusions

* Always exclude investors already in active conversations (existing relationships being worked) from cold campaign lists. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775526011064199?thread_ts=1775504033.331859&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* Exclude PE firms, VCs, Investment Banks, SPACs, and Lower Middle Market-focused investors unless the deal is explicitly appropriate for them. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775526011064199?thread_ts=1775504033.331859&cid=C09L8TFBJNP
* Conflict management: When running multiple deals simultaneously, avoid sending the same investor more than one deal at a time. Build logic to flag investors appearing across multiple deal campaigns. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1761686840279749?thread_ts=1761686840.279749&cid=C09L8TFBJNP

Investor Lead vs. Active Investor

* Active Investor = someone who has had an actual two-way dialogue — they've responded to outreach. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1776459230352849
* Investor Lead = someone in the network or pipeline who hasn't yet responded. NEVER upgrade to Active Investor based solely on outreach initiated by the team — a real response is required.
* If someone is unresponsive across 3–5 deals: do NOT keep sending them deals. Instead, identify another person at the same firm (similar role or more senior) and approach them with language like: "We've been in touch with [Name] but haven't heard back. Understanding that emails sometimes get caught in spam, we wanted to ensure this reaches someone on your team." Do NOT throw the original contact under the bus. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1771955002811799
* Reasons for non-response: person may have left the firm (email still active), hitting spam filters, ignoring emails, or too busy from too many deal sends.
* "Champion" contact at a firm = the person with the most deals associated with them — this is the correct primary contact, not whoever was arbitrarily added to the CRM. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765309324651609

Sports Deals — Specific Targeting Logic

* Sports assets are esoteric and uncorrelated — they don't behave like traditional private equity or venture investments, making them an alternative diversifier. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1776611706956959
* When targeting family offices for sports deals, look for:
    * Source of wealth (sports-adjacent industries?)
    * Background of the principal within sports, or their children involved in sports
    * Investors with other esoteric/uncorrelated assets in their portfolio
    * Investors with no current sports exposure — they may be open to adding it https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1776611706956959


5. Institutional Outreach — Communication Strategy

What Works

* Family offices get hundreds of unsolicited emails daily. They rarely engage with anyone who leads with a deal pitch on the first contact.
* What actually generates responses: proper firm introduction, track record context, never lead with "we've never met, want to invest in my deal?"
* Preferred two-approach model:
    * Approach A (warm): "I'd love to get to know you and understand how you invest. By the way, I have something that may be very relevant."
    * Approach B (direct): More deal-forward, but still grounded in proper firm introduction.
* For existing relationships (Active Investors): the email should be a proper deal pitch with attachments, sent from the advisor's personal name — not a generic sender. Recipients must recognize who the email is from. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775770786313549
* Cold outreach (Investor Leads): use plain text, no attachments, no heavy signatures, no links — these are spam triggers. Drive a response first, THEN send materials. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775770959587209
* General solicitation compliance: BD outreach must be framed as an introduction first — "we have a deal we'd love to talk about" not "here is the deal, invest now." Leading with a relationship opener keeps it compliant. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1770835043313409

Investor Lead Outreach (Cold)

* Email 1: Pure intro — no deal pitch. "I'm reaching out to introduce myself. I know you've invested in consumer deals. I'd love to get on a call and learn more about your mandate." Mention a relevant portfolio company they've invested in to show you've done research. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765433143065329
* Email 2: Follow-up bump — short, friendly.
* Email 3: More direct — "I imagine you're too busy to respond to cold requests for an intro call, so I won't waste your time. If you have an appetite for X exposure, I'd love to tell you about a deal I'm working on called Y..." https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765433143065329
* Do NOT send multiple people at the same firm at the same time — risk of them forwarding the email internally to each other, which could result in all of them declining. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765433143065329
* Deal announcement emails (e.g., announcing a closed transaction) to a 5–10K person list = a proven method to restart or generate new conversations. Works well as a blast even without opt-in, using marketing automation tools. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1763574086900989

Confidentiality Rules

* Revenue figures and company names cannot be disclosed before an NDA — especially for hot sectors like AI where information sensitivity is high. First outreach should be blind/generic on company identity. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1774993183798069
* IRRs (Internal Rates of Return) can be shared with institutional investors but NOT with individual retail investors. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1774993902129299
* Specific company names and confidential terms (e.g., specific MLB franchise names) cannot be used in outreach pre-NDA — use generic language like "a major professional sports franchise." https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1774995512655879


6. Regulatory & Compliance Framework

FINRA Rules for Broker-Dealer Outreach

* Any outreach tied to securities activities through the broker-dealer must comply with FINRA Rule 2210 — all communications must be attributed to a real, licensed person and archived. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775769035522659
* Solicitation (actively pitching an investment) can only be done by licensed individuals (FINRA-registered). Fake names/personas cannot be used for BD-related outreach.
* Archiving (via Smarsh or equivalent) is required for all broker-dealer communications — supervised and auditable by the compliance firm (Finalis).
* 10-domain limit for Smarsh-archived BD outreach — each domain must be approved and forwarding codes set up by compliance. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775162428037949
* Outlook domains: 2 domains × 25 inboxes each × 2–3 emails/day. Google domains: 6 domains × 3 inboxes each × 15–20 emails/day — per compliance-constrained infrastructure. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775595485152369
* For ERA/SPV-based outreach (HNWI, non-institutional): no archiving, no Smarsh required — lighter compliance burden.
* General solicitation risk: outreach that looks like a blatant investment solicitation "opens a can of worms." Lead with an introduction, not a pitch, to stay compliant. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1770835043313409

Two Legal Entities — Two Outreach Tracks

* MVV Capital Partners (Broker-Dealer / FINRA-registered) → for institutional outreach, must be archived and attributed to real registered persons.
* MVV Capital Management (ERA/SPV) → for HNWI / non-institutional outreach, lighter compliance, different signature.


7. Data & CRM Intelligence

Third-Party Data Sources

* FINTRX: Specialized database for family offices and RIAs. Useful for RIA coverage (45,000+ firms) with ADV/Schedule D data. However: data can be stale (firms listed under old names, firms out of business), lacks granular sub-tags (e.g., says "Technology" not "AI"), and main data is at the company level vs. contact level. Best value: RIA alternative allocation overview. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765433143065329
* Pitchbook / CapIQ: Pitchbook has different taxonomy from FINTRX (e.g., separate "aerospace" and "defense" vs. combined). CapIQ is better for public company comparables. Pitchbook needed for portfolio company matching (e.g., "find investors who own food manufacturing companies"). https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765555797840989
* Crunchbase: Good for startup/VC-stage data. Misha evaluated it — key fields needed: contact name, firm association, investment portfolio (company names), and stage at which they participated. ~50 fields max useful out of 400+ available. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1767651349564869
* FINTRX "Origin of Wealth" field = one of the most valuable fields for family office profiling — not available on most other sources. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1767652511090389
* Clay: Can pull external sources, enrich contact records, find LinkedIn profiles, validate/update emails. Useful for description overrides, portfolio enrichment, and data transformation before pushing to HubSpot.
* Parallel.ai: More flexible AI-powered search with specific natural language instructions. Example: "Find institutional investors who have experience seeding/anchoring real estate funds, only US, anchor positions only." * When integrating third-party data: create separate CRM properties per vendor — do not force incompatible taxonomies together.

Investor Profiling Best Practices

* Key fields to build per investor: Industries of interest, deal type preferences, deal type exclusions, geography, check size range (by deal type), source of wealth, rounds participated in, notable portfolio companies, EBITDA/revenue thresholds. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1770870272407979
* Deal type exclusions are as important as inclusions — some investors don't do funds, some don't do distressed, some only do control. Parsing loss/pass reasons populates this automatically. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1770870272407979
* Source of wealth (from FINTRX "Origin of Wealth") is critical for family offices — it reveals industry background which predicts sector affinity.
* For HNWI investors: track their professional background/industry (e.g., C-suite at a CPG = likely consumer-oriented). For public company executives, compensation is publicly disclosed — use that to estimate investable wealth. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1770835043313409
* Pass reason parsing: notes like "only interested in Swiss growth/venture deals" or "not deploying until next year" or "only control/cash-flowing acquisitions" should hard-exclude that investor from conflicting deals. Softer/ambiguous passes → down-rank only. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765315005366359
* Investor portfolio history (which companies + which stage they invested in) is the most actionable enrichment field — it reveals sector focus AND stage preference simultaneously.
* Email response rate (replied/delivered) is a more reliable behavioral signal than open rate — open tracking is unreliable due to firewalls and pixel loading. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1764649156273379

Relationship Intelligence

* Moving from a basic CRM to a Relationship Intelligence model is the strategic goal — understanding not just who you know but how well you know them and the best path to new targets.
* Tools like DotAlign can score relationships 1–99 based on email/calendar recency, frequency, responsiveness — enabling warm introductions instead of cold outreach. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1775096841236819
* LinkedIn integration into CRM is critical: scanning and syncing LinkedIn messages into HubSpot gives a pre-existing communication history that is more reliable than assuming a shared university connection as a reason to reach out cold. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765433143065329
* Activity signals for behavioral scoring: frequency of responses, deal stage outcomes (pass/unresponsive), reply rate across campaigns. NOT open rates (unreliable). https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1764649156273379


8. Capital Raising Operations

Deal Revenue Forecasting

* Target Capacity Units / Current Capacity Units = revenue forecast figures, not literal "units." E.g., $36M and $72M represent the projected revenue targets. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1761247717368229?thread_ts=1761247717.368229&cid=C09L8TFBJNP

Pipeline Management

* Deal pipeline naming convention: "Firm Name – Deal Name" (NOT "First Name Last Name x Deal Name"). Firm-level tracking is more appropriate than individual contact-level. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1776378045408349
* Deal stages must reflect actual investor engagement — deals only progress in the pipeline when a real response is received.
* Use "Primaries" pipeline for tracking primary deal placements.
* Deal lifecycle timing: Average deal takes 3–6 months to complete. Funds take 12–18 months depending on size, track record, and desirability of returns. No two are the same. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1772153294025499
* Deal code names (e.g., "Project Razor") must be mapped to industries manually — the system cannot auto-classify a code name. All deals should have an Industry field populated for matching to work correctly. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765477934949279
* CRM data hygiene: when uploading new investor names, existing Active Investor records must NOT be overridden by new Investor Lead uploads — lifecycle stage should never downgrade. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1765433957142119

Anchor Fund Raises (Public Equities)

* For a manager raising a $250M public equities fund, the relevant investor type is a seed/anchor fund investor — not a PE or VC investor. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1776452613789449
* Search for investors who have seeded/anchored other public equity managers as the core targeting criterion. https://controlthrive.slack.com/archives/C09L8TFBJNP/p1776452613789449
